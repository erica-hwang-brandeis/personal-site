Initial Expectations & Instruction Manual Critique

Initial Expectations: Before starting the project, I expected that the most challenging parts would be figuring out how to solder precisely and follow along with all the technical terms in the manual. Strengths of instruction manual: The entire document is very organized and fairly easy to navigate. Including photos with the step by step instructions made it easy to follow. Weaknesses of instruction manual: The instructions at the very beginning of the document were not very clear in describing what this robot car is, but I think it was mainly an issue of weird wording from translating text. The instruction sections should also include more of a prelude to the rest of the manual instead of solely focusing on the product function. What I Would Change: I would set up the beginning of the document to be more explicit in introducing the building process. I would also state the materials needed on the first page. Since the images were somewhat blurry, it was a little hard to tell which color was which. It would be more clear if there was a zoomed-in version of the metal film resistors on the side of the page so that we wouldn’t have to guess the colors.

Soldering the Electronics Kit

Being my first time ever soldering or even seeing soldering, I was a little intimidated by the high temperature and electronic aspect of the work. Starting off with the practice board made the entire project a lot less daunting as we had the opportunity to try it out multiple times to work on our technique. This helped me feel more confident starting on the actual robot.

Challenges

Our first and main bump in the road was when we soldered the two ends of the metal film resistor together. Since we twisted the wire ends as a way to keep them in place while flipping the board around, we accidentally ended up connecting the two when soldering.

Solving the issue

Luckily, we were early on in the process and it was the first resistor. We re-melted the solder with the iron then quickly used the syringe to suck up the solder so that we could disconnect the ends. Then, Professor Ben helped us use tweazers to carefully pull out the resistor to undo the damage so we can redo the soldering.

Collaborating

When collaborating on building the kits, Rebecca and I started off taking turns with each step, with one of us reading the manual while instructing the other on how to complete the step. Later on in the building process, our strengths had become more apparent, so we ended up dividing based on the type of work. The wires and orientation came more naturally to me. On the other hand, Rebecca’s soldering was neater and more visually pleasing. Our involvement in every step created great teamwork.

Measurements and Initial Design

For the measurements of the electronics kit, we use digital calipers to measure then record the numbers on a whiteboard Canva file. The drawing feature on Canva allowed us to specify what exactly we were measuring on the image of the car, instead of having to keep track of the different parts with ambiguous names. Below are the measurements and the Canva file to illustrate the measurements: Board length: 100.5 Board width: 69.7 Narrow board width: 58.1 Battery holder length: 60.2 Battery holder width: 30.1 Battery holder height: 14.8 Red light width: 6.8 Power switch height: 15.5 Power switch width: 8.4 Wheel circumference: 27.5 Wheel protruding height from board: 6.0

Part 2: Design and 3D Print a Chassis Enclosure

3D Printing

Working together to build a schematic of a design plan for a chassis enclosure for our robot using 3D printing!

3D Printed Chassis: First Iteration

We created a collaborative Fusion 360 file and divided the CAD work by brainstorming and talking through the box together while brainstorming. Later on, I finished up the details of the design on my own. While in class, Rebecca focused on slicing and producing the prints on the printers, and I spent more of my time on Fusion making edits to our design as she measured each iteration.

Slicing First Iteration

For slicing the STL in Prusa Slicer: Infill: 15% Print time: 56m

First Iteration Result

When we printed and tested the first version, the fit was snug and aligned with the car dimensions. However, it did not have any holes to make some of the features visible nor did it cover the entire car. The sides of the box were also too thin, as one edge disconnected, so we increased the thickness in our next print from 0.5mm to 1mm.

Sketching a new design

Based on these observations, we sketched a more detailed design with the new measurements and started to create it on Fusion.

The Vision

Our idea was to make it look like a horse carriage with a mustache that is popping a wheelie, in reference to its name, “Papa Wheelie.”

Second Iteration

For the second iteration, we focused on making a design that would fit around the battery holder so that it would be accessible, so the sizing was not intended to cover the entirety of the car. We also made the fitting a bit tighter so that it would not move around.

Second Iteration Print

While the box did fit, the fitting with the battery holder wasn't aligned well and had some gaps. So we adjusted the dimensions of the holes to get a more precise fit.

Third Iteration

Our final iteration incorporated the entire carriage design. We learned how to use the fillet function on Fusion to create the curved shape to resemble the back part of the carriage.

Third Iteration (Part 2)

This was the result of the third iteration. For aesthetic purposes, we decided to print it another time in white and added a lid to the opening for the battery holder. We also added short pegs on the sides to add a small 3D printed wheel, to create the visual effect of popping a wheelie.

Part 3: Laser-Cut Chassis: Second Material Test

LightBurn

Designing a parametric box for laser cutting

Designing a Parametric Box

For the last stage of this project, we learned how to design a parametric box. Referring to an instructional YouTube video, we followed along the steps to use the parameters feature on Fusion.

Parametric Practice

Instead of using the 3D printer like the rest of the enclosures, we laser cut wood to create a finger-locking joint box. Designing for laser cutting required us to set a parameter for the material thickness since the wood thickness was a set variable. Through this process, we learned how to use construction lines to draw evenly spaced rectangles to create the cut-out design for the joints.

Using LightBurn

After downloading each individual face of the box as a .dxf file, we then imported it into LightBurn to set up the laser cut.

Assembling the wooden box

Assembling the wooden enclosure was more difficult because the individual pieces needed to be glued together. The superglue took a long time to dry, so we had to hold it in the position for at least a few minutes in order for it to adhere. It was also harder to customize the laser cut design than the 3D printing design because of the assembly.

Creating a Box for Papa

Next, we moved on to design a parametric box that would fit our chassis. We went through 3 iterations to get the fit right because we had trouble with adjusting the sizing with Light Burn so the print was unintentionally scaled down for the first two iterations. We also messed up the orientation on one side, so it didn't fit with the wheels and light like it was supposed to.

Final Wooden Box

Here is the fitted version of our parametric, finger-joint box for Papa Wheelie!

Final Thoughts

This project helped me better understand how detailed and collaborative the design and prototyping process really is. I gained hands-on experience with using digital calipers for precise measurements, and I now have a much deeper appreciation for how even a small mismeasurement can affect the entire fit of a design. Learning about tolerances was also really eye-opening, just because something fits perfectly in CAD doesn’t mean it will print or cut that way in real life, so accounting for that discrepancy became an essential part of our iterations. Overall, I found this project to be challenging but fun because of the creativity aspect. I enjoyed brainstorming ideas for our Papa Wheelie’s appearance as we centered it around a theme. It pushed me to step outside of my comfort zone with new tools like Fusion 360 and soldering equipment, but it was also very rewarding to watch our design come to life—from sketch to schematic, to 3D print, to a functional wooden/PLA enclosure.
